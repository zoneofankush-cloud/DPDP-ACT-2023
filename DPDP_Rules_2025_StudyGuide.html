§ Big Picture Summary

What is this, and why should you care?

India has over 900 million internet users. Every time you sign up for an app, shop online, or use a government service, your personal data — name, phone number, location, purchase history — is collected by companies. Until now, there were no strict rules on how that data had to be protected. The DPDP Rules, 2025 change that.

These rules are the operating manual for India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023. They spell out exactly HOW companies must ask for your consent, HOW they must protect your data, WHAT they must do if there's a data breach, and HOW you can demand your data be deleted. Think of the Act as the law that says "you must drive safely" and these Rules as the traffic code that says "drive on the left, stop at red lights."

The rules apply to every business and government body in India that collects and uses personal data digitally. Non-compliance can attract massive penalties. For citizens, it means stronger rights over your own information than ever before.

Chapter 01 · Foundation

Key Players & Definitions

Before reading any law, you need to know who's who. The DPDP Rules use specific terms for specific people. Once you know these four players, everything else clicks into place.

Data Principal [DP]
That's YOU — the individual whose personal data is being collected. The word "principal" means the main person who owns something. You own your data.
Data Fiduciary [DF]
The company or organisation that decides WHY and HOW your data is used. "Fiduciary" means someone who holds something in trust. So a Data Fiduciary holds your data in trust — like Zomato, HDFC Bank, or Amazon India.
Data Processor [DP2]
A third party that processes data on behalf of the Data Fiduciary. Example: Zomato (Fiduciary) might use an AWS cloud server (Processor) to store your order data. AWS doesn't decide what to do with your data — Zomato does.
Data Protection Board [the Board]
India's new data police — a government body that can investigate complaints, order companies to fix violations, and impose penalties.
🏥
Think of it like this

You are a patient (Data Principal). The hospital (Data Fiduciary) collects your health records and decides what to do with them. A medical lab they outsource tests to (Data Processor) only processes samples — they follow hospital instructions. And the Medical Council (the Board) watches over the whole system.

When do these Rules kick in?

Not all rules start at once. The government has given businesses time to prepare:

Day 1 — November 13, 2025
Basic rules (Rules 1, 2, 17–21) come into force immediately — covering Board formation and governance.
Year 1 — November 2026
Rule 4 (Consent Managers) comes into force — businesses that want to operate as consent intermediaries must register.
18 Months — May 2027
All remaining rules (3, 5–16, 22–23) come into force — the full notice, consent, security, children's data, and breach rules.

Chapter 02 · Notice & Consent

The Right to Know Before You Say Yes

Rule 3 is about the notice a company must give you BEFORE it asks for your consent to use your data. This is the "informed" in informed consent. No more vague, 50-page terms you never read.

What must a notice contain?

Every notice must be standalone and understandable on its own — it cannot assume you've read something else. In plain language, it must tell you:

  • Exactly which pieces of your data are being collected (e.g., "your name, email, and purchase history" — not just "personal information")
  • The specific purpose(s) for which data is collected (e.g., "to deliver your order" — not "to improve our services")
  • A direct link to the website/app where you can withdraw consent, exercise your rights, or file a complaint with the Board
🌍 Real World Example

BAD (what companies used to do): "By using this app you agree to our Privacy Policy." — vague, buried in a link, no specifics.

GOOD (what Rule 3 requires): "We will collect your name, mobile number, and delivery address to process your food order. [Withdraw consent here] [Exercise your rights here] [File a complaint here]."

Withdrawing Consent

A critical provision: withdrawing consent must be as easy as giving it. If you can opt-in with one tap, you must be able to opt-out with one tap. No hiding the unsubscribe button in grey text at the bottom of a page.

📋
Think of it like this

Imagine a landlord asking you to sign a rental agreement. Rule 3 says: they can't just hand you 40 pages of legal text and say "sign here." They must explain in plain language what you're agreeing to, and if you want to break the lease, the process must be as easy as the process to sign it.

Chapter 03 · New Concept

Consent Managers — Your Personal Data Butler

Rule 4 introduces a brand new type of entity to India's digital landscape: the Consent Manager. This is a regulated, registered intermediary that acts as your personal dashboard for managing all your data consents across multiple companies.

🔑
Think of it like this

Think of a Consent Manager like a master key app on your phone. Instead of managing 50 separate app permissions across 50 different settings menus, you manage everything from ONE place. Give consent to Bank A. Revoke permission from App B. See who has your data. All in one dashboard.

Who can become a Consent Manager?

Not just anyone. To register, a company must:

  • Be incorporated in India
  • Have a net worth of at least ₹2 crore
  • Have sound financial management, good character directors, and no conflicts of interest with Data Fiduciaries
  • Be independently certified that their platform meets data protection standards
  • Include provisions in their Articles of Association that can only be changed with Board approval

What are a Consent Manager's duties?

ObligationWhat it means in practice
Enable consentLet you give, review, or withdraw consent to any company on their platform
Blind to contentThey route your data sharing but cannot read the actual data
Keep recordsMaintain a log of all consents, notices, and data sharings for at least 7 years
No sub-contractingCannot outsource their core duties to another party
No conflictsDirectors/management cannot have financial stakes in Data Fiduciaries
TransparencyMust publicly disclose ownership, shareholders above 2%, and related companies
🌍 Real World Example

Scenario: Priya uses ConsentHub (a fictional Consent Manager). Bank B1 wants access to her salary slips held by Bank B2. Through ConsentHub, Priya approves the transfer with one tap. ConsentHub routes the instruction to B2, who sends the data to B1. ConsentHub itself never sees the salary data — it just manages the consent transaction, like a notary public.

Chapter 04 · Security

Protecting Data — and What Happens When Things Go Wrong

Rules 6, 7, and 8 form the security backbone of the DPDP Rules. Rule 6 sets out what safeguards companies MUST have. Rule 7 covers what they must do if data is breached. Rule 8 governs how long they can keep your data.

Rule 6 — Minimum Security Standards

Every Data Fiduciary must protect data using at least these measures:

MeasurePlain EnglishReal Example
EncryptionScramble the data so only authorised systems can read itYour bank password stored as a hash, not plain text
Access ControlOnly certain people/systems can access certain dataA customer service rep can't access payment card CVV
Audit LogsKeep a record of who accessed what and whenA log showing "admin user X downloaded 500 records at 2am"
BackupsKeep copies so data isn't permanently lost if attackedDaily encrypted database backups to a separate server
Log RetentionKeep access logs for at least 1 yearSecurity logs retained from Jan 2026 to Jan 2027

Rule 7 — The 72-Hour Clock

If there's a data breach, the company faces TWO parallel notification obligations:

⏱️
Think of it like this

Imagine a water pipe in your apartment building bursts. The building manager must tell each affected resident immediately ("your flat is flooded, here's what to do"). AND they must tell the municipal authority with a full report within 72 hours. Both notifications are mandatory — you can't do only one.

🌍 Real World Example — Breach Notification

Scenario: BigCart (an e-commerce platform) discovers on a Monday that hackers accessed 10 lakh customer email addresses and delivery addresses over the weekend.

To YOU (the user): Immediately — via your registered email/SMS — "Your account was part of a breach. Your email and delivery address may be exposed. Please change your password. [Contact our support here]"

To the Board: A preliminary report immediately + a detailed report by Thursday (72 hours), including: what was breached, why it happened, who likely caused it, and what remedial steps have been taken.

Rule 8 — Data Erasure: The "Delete When Done" Rule

Companies cannot keep your data forever just because they have it. If you stop using a service and neither contact them nor use your rights, they must erase your data after a set period.

Type of CompanySize ThresholdErasure After
E-commerce Entity2 crore+ registered users3 years of inactivity
Online Gaming Platform50 lakh+ registered users3 years of inactivity
Social Media Platform2 crore+ registered users3 years of inactivity
All Data FiduciariesAny sizeMinimum 1 year retention for logs, then erase
⚠ Important Detail

48 hours before erasing your data, the company MUST send you a warning: "We are about to delete your data. Log in or contact us to stop this." This gives you a final chance to re-engage before permanent deletion.

Chapter 05 · Special Protections

Children's Data — Extra Walls, Extra Care

Rules 10, 11, and 12 create a special protection layer for children (under 18) and persons with disabilities. The principle: children cannot meaningfully consent to data processing themselves, so a verified parent or legal guardian must do it for them.

Rule 10 — Verifiable Parental Consent

Before processing any child's data, a company must get verifiable consent from a parent — not just a checkbox saying "I confirm I am over 18." The parent must be identifiable as an actual adult.

A company can verify the parent is an adult through one of these methods:

  • Identity/age details the parent already shared with the platform (e.g., they're a registered user with verified KYC)
  • Identity details voluntarily provided by the parent (Aadhaar, passport etc.)
  • A virtual token issued by an authorised entity (e.g., DigiLocker-verified token mapped to age proof)
🌍 Real World Example — 4 Scenarios

Scenario A: 14-year-old Riya wants to join an educational app. She says her mum is her parent. Mum is already a registered user with verified age on the platform. ✅ Platform can verify mum is an adult — consent obtained.

Scenario B: Riya's mum is NOT registered. The platform asks mum to share an Aadhaar/DigiLocker token to confirm she's 18+. ✅ Verification happens digitally without mum needing to be a prior user.

Scenario C: A parent opens a new account for their child directly. Same rules apply — verify the parent's age first, then create the child account.

Rule 12 — When Are Parental Consent Rules Relaxed?

Some situations don't require the full parental consent process. These exemptions exist because requiring parent verification every time would actually harm children:

Who is ExemptAllowed Purpose Only
Hospitals / DoctorsOnly to provide health services to the child
Schools / Educational InstitutionsOnly for educational activities or child safety monitoring
Creches / DaycareOnly to track/monitor children in their care for safety
School transport operatorsOnly to track real-time location during travel
Any Data FiduciaryCreating email-only accounts (limited to email communication)
Any Data FiduciaryReal-time location tracking for safety/protection of the child
🏫
Think of it like this

A school doesn't need to call every parent before taking attendance. But a gaming company does need parent sign-off before a 13-year-old creates an account and starts spending in-game currency. The exemptions are narrowly defined — they protect children's welfare, not company convenience.

Chapter 06 · Your Rights

Data Principal Rights — What You Can Demand

Rule 14 is the empowerment rule. It doesn't just grant you rights — it tells companies exactly HOW they must enable you to exercise them. Rights without a mechanism are meaningless. This rule creates the mechanism.

What companies must publish (Rule 9 & 14)

  • On their website/app: how to make a request to exercise your rights (access, correction, deletion etc.)
  • A contact person (Data Protection Officer or equivalent) who can answer your questions
  • A grievance redressal system that must respond within 90 days

Your right to Nominate

You can nominate another person (family member, trusted friend) to exercise your data rights on your behalf — similar to how you can give power of attorney. This is especially useful for elderly or differently-abled individuals.

Cross-Border Data Transfer (Rule 15)

Your data CAN be sent outside India — but only if the Central Government has approved it for that specific foreign country or entity. The government can restrict transfer to hostile countries or unreliable entities.

🌍 Real World Example

You want to delete your account and all associated data from an e-commerce platform. Under Rule 14, the platform must have a clearly visible option on the app to initiate this. They cannot make you send a letter by post. They cannot make you call a call centre and wait on hold. The process must be digital and accessible. And they must respond within 90 days.

Research & Statistics Exemption (Rule 16)

Academic research, public health studies, and statistical analysis are exempt from most DPDP obligations — but ONLY if conducted according to the standards in the Second Schedule (lawful processing, data minimisation, security safeguards etc.).

🔬
Think of it like this

A hospital can use anonymised patient data to study cancer patterns without asking each of 10,000 patients for individual consent — but they must follow strict research ethics, use only what's necessary, and protect the data rigorously. The exemption is for public benefit, not a loophole.

Chapter 07 · Big Players

Significant Data Fiduciaries — Bigger Company, Bigger Responsibility

Rule 13 creates a special category called "Significant Data Fiduciary" (SDF) — companies that handle such massive volumes of sensitive data that their failures could affect millions of people. Think of Meta, Google, large banks, or telecom companies operating in India.

Extra obligations for SDFs:

ObligationWhat it meansFrequency
Data Protection Impact AssessmentA formal study of how their data processing might risk users' rightsEvery 12 months
AuditAn independent review of whether they're following the ActEvery 12 months
Algorithm ReviewCheck that AI/software used to process data doesn't risk users' rightsOngoing due diligence
Data LocalisationCertain sensitive data specified by the government CANNOT leave IndiaPer government order
⚠ Data Localisation — Why It Matters

Rule 13(4) gives the government power to designate certain types of data as "stay in India" data. For example, if the government decides that Aadhaar-linked records, financial data, or health records must not be stored on foreign servers, SDFs must comply. This is India's answer to concerns about foreign governments accessing Indian citizens' data.

🏦
Think of it like this

A small neighbourhood kirana store and HDFC Bank both have to follow food safety or financial regulations — but HDFC has far more compliance obligations, regular RBI audits, and stricter capital requirements. SDFs are the HDFC Banks of the data world — more power, more scrutiny.

Chapter 08 · Governance

The Board, Appeals & How It All Works

Rules 17–23 govern the Data Protection Board itself — how it's formed, how it runs, how you appeal its decisions, and how the government can request data from companies. This is the enforcement machinery.

Forming the Board (Rules 17–19)

The Board is led by a Chairperson and several Members, selected by special Search-cum-Selection Committees. Key governance features:

  • A Cabinet Secretary-led committee recommends the Chairperson
  • One-third of members form a quorum — decisions are by majority vote
  • Chairperson gets a casting vote in case of a tie
  • Members with a conflict of interest on any matter must recuse themselves
  • Board has 6 months to complete inquiries (extendable by 3 months at a time)

The Board as a Digital Office (Rule 20)

Remarkably, the Board operates entirely digitally. You can file complaints, attend hearings, and receive orders — all online, without physically going to any office. This is rare for Indian regulatory bodies.

Salary of Board Members

Chairperson: ₹4.5 lakh/month (consolidated, no house/car). Members: ₹4 lakh/month. No pension, no gratuity — it's a time-bound appointment.

Appeals (Rule 22)

Not happy with a Board decision? You can appeal to the Appellate Tribunal. The appeal must be filed digitally, with a fee (same as TRAI appeal fees). The Tribunal is also a digital office and follows principles of natural justice rather than rigid civil procedure.

Government's Power to Demand Data (Rule 23)

The Central Government can require any Data Fiduciary to share user data for purposes like national security or sovereignty. In sensitive cases, the government can also order the company not to tell you that your data was shared.

⚠ Privacy Note on Rule 23

This is the most controversial provision. It allows government agencies to access personal data and suppress disclosure for security reasons. Critics argue this needs stronger safeguards; supporters argue it's necessary for national security. It's a tension that exists in data laws globally.

⚖️
Think of it like this

The Board is like a consumer court for data disputes — except it works online, decides faster, and handles tens of thousands of cases. The Appellate Tribunal is the High Court above it. And Rule 23 is like the government's warrant power — legal but regulated.

§ Summary

10 Golden Takeaways

01

You own your data. You are the Data Principal. Companies hold your data in trust — not as their property.

02

Consent must be specific. Vague, all-purpose consent notices are no longer acceptable. Companies must tell you exactly what they're taking and why.

03

Opt-out must equal opt-in. If subscribing takes one click, unsubscribing must also take one click.

04

72 hours = the breach deadline. Companies have 72 hours to give the Board a full breach report.

05

Your data has an expiry. Large platforms must erase inactive user data after 3 years. All companies must keep logs for at least 1 year.

06

Children get special protection. Verified parental consent is needed before processing any child's data — not just a checkbox.

07

Consent Managers are a new power tool. A single regulated dashboard to manage all your data consents across all platforms.

08

Big companies face stricter rules. Significant Data Fiduciaries must do annual impact assessments, audits, and may face data localisation requirements.

09

Everything is digital. The Board, complaints, hearings, appeals — all online. No need to go to a physical office.

10

Full rollout by May 2027. Most rules don't apply until 18 months after November 2025. Businesses have a compliance window.
